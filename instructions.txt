Online Polling Platform
1. Brief Description
Teams and communities frequently need quick, structured input on a question — the date for an
off-site, the next feature to prioritise, the topic for the next book-club pick. When that input lives
in chat threads or back-and-forth email, opinions get lost, double-counted, or quietly ignored.
The task is to build an online polling platform where any authenticated user can create a poll,
configure how it should behave (single-choice or multi-choice; whether it is public to the team or
restricted to specific invitees; whether results are visible before voting; when it should close),
publish it for others to vote on, and review aggregate results once enough people have
responded.
Polls can be shared by URL. Public polls show up in the team’s public feed; private polls do not
appear in the feed and only invited users can find them — even by guessing the link. Voting is
attributed under the hood (so the system can enforce one vote per person per poll) but
individual choices are kept private — other viewers see only aggregate counts and percentages.
The goal is a working end-to-end product. Correct lifecycle handling, correct enforcement of the
one-vote-per-user rule, and correct results-visibility behaviour matter more than visual polish or
features beyond what is specified.
Context &amp; constraints
• Time-box: 6 hours of focused build time.
• AI assistance is expected. Any AI coding assistant (Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code,
ChatGPT, etc.) may be used freely. Scope and quality expectations are calibrated to AI-
assisted development.
• Tech stack: fully open. Any language, framework, or storage layer is acceptable.
• UI polish is welcomed but secondary — clarity, correctness of lifecycle and vote
enforcement, and a clean data model are primary.
• Deliverable: a public GitHub repository with a README (see Submission section).
2. Business Requirements
• BR1. Allow any authenticated user to create a poll with a question, two or more options,
and configurable behaviour (single-choice or multi-choice, public or private visibility, when
results are visible, when the poll closes).
• BR2. Allow polls to move through a clear lifecycle from Draft (creator editing) to Open
(accepting votes) to Closed (results frozen).
• BR3. Support two visibility modes: Public polls are discoverable in the team feed by all
authenticated users; Private polls are accessible only to users explicitly invited by the
creator, and never appear in the public feed.
• BR4. Each poll has a stable shareable URL the creator can copy and distribute. Knowing
the URL alone does not grant access to a private poll — the user must also be invited.
• BR5. Allow any authenticated user with access to an Open poll to vote on it.
• BR6. Enforce one vote-set per user per poll: voters may change their selection while the
poll is Open, but cannot inflate or fragment counts.
• BR7. Surface aggregate results (per-option counts and percentages) on each poll.
Individual voters’ choices remain private.

• BR8. Support a results-visibility setting per poll so the creator can choose whether results
are visible to everyone who can access the poll immediately or only after they have voted.
• BR9. Allow polls to be discovered through a browseable list with sensible filters and sort,
scoped to what the viewer is entitled to see.
• BR10. Authenticate users and prevent tampering with poll configuration, vote counts, the
invitee list, or other users’ vote choices.
• BR11. Persist all data reliably so polls, votes, invitations, and results are not lost between
sessions or restarts.
• BR12. Scale gracefully as the catalogue of polls grows, so list and detail views remain
usable.
3. Functional Requirements
3.1 Authentication
• FR1. Users can register a new account by providing a name, email, and password.
• FR2. Users can sign in with their email and password.
• FR3. Users can sign out, ending their session.
• FR4. All polling-related screens and data are accessible only to authenticated users;
unauthenticated requests are redirected to sign-in or rejected.
• FR5. Passwords must be stored hashed (e.g. bcrypt, argon2, scrypt). Plaintext or
reversibly-encrypted password storage is not acceptable.
3.2 Poll management
• FR6. An authenticated user can create a Poll. A Poll captures: title, optional description,
type (Single-choice or Multi-choice), two or more options (each a text label), visibility
(Public or Private), results-visibility setting (Always visible or Visible after voting), optional
end-at datetime, share-token (an unguessable URL-safe identifier used in the shareable
link), creator, status, created-at, updated-at.
• FR7. A newly created Poll has status Draft.
• FR8. The creator of a Draft poll can edit any of its fields, including the option list, the type,
and the visibility.
• FR9. The creator can delete a Draft poll. Deletion is irreversible.
• FR10. Draft polls are visible only to their creator regardless of visibility setting.
3.3 Poll lifecycle
• FR11. Status is one of: Draft, Open, Closed.
• FR12. The creator can publish a Draft poll, transitioning it to Open. Publishing requires at
least 2 options. After publishing, the poll’s options, type, and visibility are locked and
cannot be modified.
• FR13. After publishing, the creator may extend the end-at to a later time but cannot move
it earlier than the current moment. The creator may also close the poll manually at any
time, transitioning it to Closed.
• FR14. When a poll has an end-at and that time has passed, the poll must transition to
Closed. Auto-closing on the next read or write touching the poll is acceptable; a
background scheduler is not required.
• FR15. A Closed poll cannot be reopened, edited, or voted on. Once Closed, the poll is
read-only.

• FR16. Once a poll is published, it cannot be deleted; the creator may close it but the
historical poll and its votes are preserved.
3.4 Visibility and shareable links
• FR17. Every poll has a stable shareable URL containing an unguessable share-token (e.g.
a 16+ character URL-safe random string). The token must be generated on poll creation
and remain stable for the life of the poll.
• FR18. Public polls: any authenticated user can access the poll detail and may vote (if the
poll is Open). Public polls appear in the public feed (see 3.7).
• FR19. Private polls: only the creator and explicitly invited users can access the poll detail
or vote. Private polls do NOT appear in the public feed under any filter.
• FR20. Access enforcement applies on every read and write touching a private poll —
detail view, results, voting, and any related list — not just the listing. An authenticated user
who is not invited and not the creator must not be able to view a private poll, even when
supplied with the correct share URL.
• FR21. An authenticated user with the correct share URL can reach the poll directly. For a
public poll, the URL grants access. For a private poll, the URL grants access only if the
user is the creator or an invitee; otherwise the user sees a clear “you do not have access
to this poll” message (and not the poll’s title or details).
3.5 Invitations (private polls only)
• FR22. The creator of a private poll can invite an existing registered user by email.
Invitations capture: poll, invited user, invited-by (= creator), invited-at.
• FR23. The creator can revoke an invitation at any time. Revocation immediately removes
the invitee’s access. If the revoked user had already voted, their vote remains counted; the
historical vote is not deleted but the user can no longer view the poll detail or change their
vote. The README should make this behaviour explicit.
• FR24. An invitee list (showing invited users, invited-at, and who invited them) is visible to
the creator of the private poll. It is not visible to invitees or other users.
• FR25. Invitations may be added while the poll is in any status (Draft, Open, Closed).
• FR26. Invitations apply only to private polls; attempts to add invitees to a public poll must
be rejected with a clear message.
3.6 Voting
• FR27. An authenticated user with access to an Open poll can vote on it. For a Single-
choice poll the user selects exactly one option; for a Multi-choice poll the user selects one
or more options.
• FR28. A user has at most one vote-set per poll. Submitting a vote on a poll the user has
already voted on must replace the previous selection rather than create an additional vote.
• FR29. A user cannot vote on a Draft or Closed poll. A user without access to a private poll
cannot vote on it. Attempts via the UI must be prevented; attempts via the API must be
rejected.
• FR30. A user can withdraw their vote on an Open poll, removing their selections. Once
withdrawn, the user may vote again before the poll closes.
• FR31. Individual voters’ choices must not be visible to other users (including the creator).
Each user can see their own vote; aggregate counts are public to everyone who can
access the poll (subject to the results-visibility rule).
3.7 Results

• FR32. A poll’s results display, per option, the count of votes received and the percentage
of respondents who selected it. A total-respondents figure (count of distinct users who
voted) is also displayed.
• FR33. For Single-choice polls the per-option percentages sum to 100%. For Multi-choice
polls each option’s percentage is computed against total respondents, so percentages
may sum to more than 100% — this is expected behaviour and should be acknowledged
in the UI.
• FR34. When the poll’s results-visibility setting is “Always visible,” any viewer with access
to the poll sees results regardless of whether they have voted.
• FR35. When the setting is “Visible after voting,” only users with access who have voted
(and the poll’s creator) may see results while the poll is Open. Other viewers see a clear
prompt explaining they must vote to see results.
• FR36. Once a poll is Closed, results are visible to all viewers with access regardless of the
results-visibility setting.
3.8 Listing, filtering, sorting, pagination
• FR37. The public feed shows Open and Closed PUBLIC polls only. Drafts are excluded
from public lists; private polls never appear in the public feed under any filter.
• FR38. A “My Polls” view shows polls created by the current user (all statuses, all
visibilities) and is visible only to that user.
• FR39. A “Shared with me” view shows private polls (Open or Closed) where the current
user is an invitee but not the creator.
• FR40. The public feed can be filtered by status (Open / Closed) and sorted by created-at,
end-at, and total respondents, both ascending and descending.
• FR41. All long list views must be paginated with a sensible default page size (e.g. 20).
Pagination controls must be visible and functional; filters and sort apply across the
paginated set.
3.9 Persistence
• FR42. All data — users, polls, options, votes, invitations — must persist across application
restarts. Any storage approach is acceptable provided the choice is justified in the
README.
3.10 Validation
• FR43. Poll title is required and must not be empty or whitespace-only.
• FR44. A poll must have at least two options. Each option label is required and not empty
or whitespace-only.
• FR45. End-at, when set, must be in the future at the moment of publishing.
• FR46. A vote submission must reference option IDs that belong to the poll being voted on.
Submissions referencing other polls’ options must be rejected.
• FR47. A vote on a Single-choice poll must include exactly one option ID. A vote on a Multi-
choice poll must include one or more option IDs. Mismatches must be rejected with a clear
message.
• FR48. Invitation emails must reference an existing registered user. Attempts to invite a
non-existent email should surface a clear message (or, optionally, a separate explicit
invite-by-email-only flow if the candidate prefers to model deferred invitations).
• FR49. Email addresses provided at registration must be syntactically valid and unique
within the system.
• FR50. All validation failures must surface clear, actionable messages.

4. Acceptance Criteria
• AC1. A new user can register, sign out, and sign back in successfully. Passwords are
stored hashed (verifiable by inspecting the storage or code).
• AC2. An unauthenticated visitor cannot reach any application screen or API endpoint
without being redirected to sign-in or rejected.
• AC3. A user can create a Draft poll with title, two or more options, a chosen type (Single or
Multi), a chosen visibility (Public or Private), a chosen results-visibility setting, and an
optional end-at.
• AC4. Every poll has a stable shareable URL containing an unguessable token. The same
URL works for the life of the poll.
• AC5. The creator can edit any field of their own Draft poll, including changing visibility
between Public and Private.
• AC6. The creator can delete their own Draft poll; it disappears from their dashboard.
• AC7. The creator can publish a Draft (status becomes Open). After publishing, attempts to
change the options, type, or visibility are rejected with a clear message.
• AC8. After publishing, the creator can extend the end-at to a later time, but cannot move it
earlier than the current moment. The creator can manually close the poll at any time.
• AC9. An Open poll whose end-at has passed transitions to Closed when next accessed.
Once Closed, attempts to vote or to modify the poll are rejected with a clear message.
• AC10. A public poll appears in the public feed for all authenticated users. Another
authenticated user can open it via the feed or the share URL and (if entitled by results-
visibility) view results.
• AC11. A private poll does NOT appear in the public feed under any filter or sort, even for
authenticated users.
• AC12. An authenticated user who is neither the creator nor an invitee of a private poll
cannot access the poll detail, results, or vote endpoint — even when given the correct
share URL. The response shows a clear “no access” message that does not reveal the
poll’s title or options.
• AC13. The creator of a private poll can invite an existing registered user by email; the
invitee then sees the poll on their “Shared with me” view and can access the detail via the
share URL.
• AC14. The creator of a private poll can revoke an invitation. The revoked user can no
longer access the poll detail, vote, or change their vote. If they had already voted, that vote
remains counted.
• AC15. Attempts to add invitations to a public poll, or to invite a non-registered email, are
rejected with a clear message.
• AC16. An authenticated user with access can vote on a Single-choice Open poll by
selecting exactly one option. Submitting fewer or more than one option is rejected with a
clear message.
• AC17. An authenticated user with access can vote on a Multi-choice Open poll by
selecting one or more options. Submitting zero options is rejected with a clear message.
• AC18. A user resubmitting a vote on a poll they have already voted on replaces their prior
selection; total respondents and option counts reflect the new selection, not both.
• AC19. A user can withdraw their vote on an Open poll, restoring them to a non-voter state.
The poll counts decrement accordingly.
• AC20. With results-visibility “Always visible,” any viewer with access to an Open poll sees
the counts and percentages.
• AC21. With results-visibility “Visible after voting,” a viewer with access who has not voted
sees a “vote to see results” prompt and not the counts; a viewer who has voted sees the

results; the creator always sees results. After the poll closes, all viewers with access see
results.
• AC22. Results display correct counts and percentages. For a Single-choice poll, per-
option percentages sum to 100%. For a Multi-choice poll, the total-respondents figure is
shown separately and per-option percentages may sum to more than 100%.
• AC23. A user cannot vote on another user’s behalf, cannot tamper with counts via direct
API calls, and cannot view another user’s individual selection.
• AC24. The public feed, “My Polls,” and “Shared with me” views each return only the
entries the viewer is entitled to see, support filtering and sorting as specified, and paginate
correctly.
• AC25. All data persists after stopping and restarting the application.
5. Submission Requirements
The candidate must submit a link to a public GitHub repository. The repository must include a
README that contains the following sections.
README must include
• Setup &amp; run instructions. Any reviewer should be able to clone the repo and run the app
locally in under 10 minutes.
• Tech stack &amp; rationale. What was chosen and why, particularly the storage layer and the
auth/password-hashing approach.
• Architectural overview. A short description of how the code is organized (layers,
modules, key files).
• How AI tools were used. Which AI assistants were used; which parts of the codebase
were AI-generated, AI-assisted, or hand-written; what was reviewed, edited, or rejected;
anything surprising or worth flagging.
• Assumptions. Anything assumed about scope, users, or environment.
• Trade-offs. What was deliberately deprioritized and why, given the 6-hour budget.
• Future work. What would be added or improved with more time.
Required deliverables (beyond the README)
• Incremental commit history. Commits should be incremental and meaningful so that
reviewers can see how the work progressed. Single-commit submissions are discouraged.
What is not required
• Production deployment. Local-run is sufficient.
• Unauthenticated / public-link voting. All voters must be signed-in users; the share URL
controls discoverability, not authentication.
• Password-protected polls or per-vote PINs. The Public/Private distinction with explicit
invitee lists is sufficient.
• Multi-question surveys. Each poll has exactly one question.
• Real-time updates (websockets / live result refresh). Results refresh on page reload.
• Charts or visualisations beyond simple bar / percent display.
• Comments or discussion on polls.
• Truly anonymous voting (votes are attributed internally for the one-vote-per-user rule, but
never exposed to other users).
• Email, SMS, or in-app push notifications — including invitation-acceptance notifications.
• CSV or other export of results.

• Scheduled-start polls (a poll opens immediately when published; future-start is out of
scope).
• Bulk inviting by uploading a CSV. Inviting one email at a time is sufficient.
• Group / team / role-based invitations. Invitees are individual registered users.
• Multi-tenant or organisation-level separation.
What is welcomed but optional
• Bar or pie-chart visualisations of results.
• CSV export of results, scoped to the viewer’s access (only their own polls or polls they
were invited to).
• Bulk-inviting multiple users at once (comma-separated emails or selecting from a user list).
• A simple notification panel that lists new invitations the user has received.
• Future-start scheduling (a Draft becomes Open automatically at a chosen time).
• Truly anonymous votes (no user attribution stored, with a different mechanism for the one-
vote-per-user rule).
• Containerization (Dockerfile, docker-compose).
• Keyboard shortcuts, dark mode, or other UX niceties.
